# Dawn's Fire

*A follower's account — an unnamed acolyte of **Sarenrae**, of the temple **Varic Sarian** raised in Drezen, written a month into the rebuilding.*

### The Call

I have no name worth recording in histories. I am one of dozens who answered Sosiel Vaenic's call to build a temple in Drezen. But I write this account because I believe the small stories matter too — how ordinary people come to follow extraordinary ones.

I was an acolyte at the temple of Sarenrae in Egede, content with healing minor injuries and teaching children their prayers, when word came of the miracle at Kenabres. A half-elven priest named Varic Sarian had helped destroy a wardstone before demons could corrupt it, and in that moment had been touched by divine power — not just by the Dawnflower, but by Iomedae herself.

The senior priests whispered about it in hushed tones. A priest of healing who had embraced the purifying flame. One who understood that mercy without justice was incomplete, that some corruptions required fire to cleanse.

When I heard he was establishing a temple in the reclaimed fortress of Drezen, I knew where I needed to be.

### The Journey

The caravan to Drezen carried thirty of us — priests, acolytes, and craftsmen from various faiths. I traveled with two other Sarenites: Brother Tomás, an old healer who'd served through three crusades, and Sister Elara, barely older than myself, whose gift for architectural design would help shape the new temple.

Brother Tomás had actually met Varic once, years ago.

"He came through on pilgrimage," Tomás told us one evening by the campfire. "Young priest, maybe twenty-five summers. Skilled healer, but there was something else. When we had a prisoner — a cultist who'd poisoned a well — most of us wanted to show mercy, offer redemption. But Varic... he looked at that man and saw only the seventeen dead children. He argued for execution with such controlled fury that even our High Dawn had to pause."

"Did they execute him?" Elara asked.

"No. We offered redemption, as is our way. The cultist escaped two days later and burned a granary. Three more died in the fire." Tomás stared into the flames. "I think about that sometimes. About whether mercy without wisdom is truly merciful."

### First Glimpse

I first saw Varic Sarian directing the temple's foundation work. He stood in simple priest's robes — no ostentation despite his newfound power — but there was something different about him. The way sunlight seemed to gather around him wasn't entirely natural. When he turned to address a question about the altar placement, I caught a glimpse of his eyes.

They held fire. Not metaphorically — actual flames danced in the depths of his amber eyes, controlled but present.

He noticed our arrival and approached with a warm smile that seemed genuine despite the weight he clearly carried.

"Welcome, brothers and sisters," he said, his voice carrying the accent of Augustana's coast. "Your timing is perfect. We're about to consecrate the ground."

Sister Elara stepped forward with the architectural plans she'd drawn. "Father Varic, I've brought designs that might—"

"Please," he interrupted gently, "just Varic. We're all servants here." He examined her plans with genuine interest. "These are excellent. You've included alcoves for seven faiths?"

"Eight, actually," Elara replied proudly. "I thought we should include a space for Shelyn. Beauty and art matter even — especially — in dark places."

Something passed across his face — surprise, then deep appreciation. "You're absolutely right. Thank you for thinking of what I overlooked."

### The Consecration

That evening, as the sun touched the horizon, Varic led the consecration ritual. Representatives from five faiths had gathered — Sarenrae, Iomedae, Torag, Desna, and Abadar. Each would speak their blessing in turn.

When Varic stepped forward for Sarenrae's blessing, he paused, looking at the assembled group. The setting sun painted everything in shades of gold and crimson.

"Before I speak the blessing," he said, "I should tell you something. For years, I served the Dawnflower through healing and glory alone. I turned away from her aspect of purifying fire, fearing my own anger at those who had taken my family."

The crowd grew silent. This wasn't how consecrations usually began.

"But in facing the demons at Kenabres, in seeing corruption that reveled in its evil, I learned something. Mercy without the possibility of judgment is not mercy — it's enablement. Healing without the will to cauterize infection is incomplete."

He raised his hand, and flame danced across his fingers — not the orange of normal fire, but the pure gold of divine light.

"This temple will shelter all who seek redemption. It will heal all who suffer. But it will also stand as a beacon against the darkness, and when corruption comes that cannot be redeemed, we will not hesitate to burn it away."

He spoke the consecration then, in the old tongue, and the ground itself seemed to warm beneath our feet.

> "Let this ground be sanctified by the dawn's first light, let healing flow like morning dew upon the wounded, let glory shine forth in righteous deeds, and let holy fire consume all that would corrupt this sacred space."

### Working Together

Over the following weeks, I worked closely with the other acolytes, laying stones and mixing mortar. Varic worked alongside us, never asking anything he wouldn't do himself. During breaks, he would heal our minor injuries — cuts, bruises, the aches of hard labor — with a touch that carried unusual warmth.

One day, while I struggled with a particularly heavy stone, he appeared beside me. Together, we lifted it into place.

"You're from Egede's temple," he said. Not a question.

"Yes, Father — I mean, Varic. How did you know?"

"The way you perform the morning prayers. Each temple has subtle variations." He smiled. "I studied there once, briefly. Beautiful library."

I gathered my courage. "Brother Tomás told us about your visit. About the cultist."

His expression darkened slightly. "Ah. Yes. I was young and certain I knew best. Pride in a different form."

"But you were right, weren't you? He did kill again."

Varic was quiet for a moment, his hand resting on the stone we'd just placed. "Being right and being wise aren't always the same thing. I was right about the danger, but wrong in how I presented it. I made it about my anger rather than about protecting innocents. The High Dawn sensed that, I think."

He looked at me directly, and I saw the flames in his eyes flicker. "The hardest lesson I've learned is that righteous anger must be tempered by purpose. Fire that burns without direction only creates more ash."

### The Unexpected Community

Three weeks into construction, something remarkable began to happen. The temple site became more than just a construction project — it became the heart of Drezen's renewal.

Refugees would gather in the evenings to watch us work. At first, they kept their distance, but Sister Elara had the brilliant idea to set up a communal kitchen using one of the completed alcoves. Soon, families were sharing meals in the shadow of the rising walls. A baker from Kenabres — her shop destroyed in the demon attack — began making bread using our ovens. A seamstress set up a temporary stall to mend clothes for workers.

I watched this miniature marketplace grow day by day. These weren't followers of any particular faith, just people desperate for normalcy in an abnormal place. The temple grounds became safe neutral territory where former enemies could trade without fear.

One evening, I saw a Mendevian soldier bargaining with a merchant who I suspected had once smuggled goods for the other side. They haggled over the price of lamp oil while their children played together with wooden blocks Brother Tomás had carved.

This was what we were really building — not just walls and altars, but a place where community could take root.

### The Garden

A young woman named Asha arrived in the fourth week, carrying a small bag of seeds. She was neither priest nor merchant, just a farmer's daughter from the south whose family had fled the advancing corruption.

"I know nothing grows properly in the Worldwound," she said to anyone who would listen, "but blessed ground might be different."

She asked permission to try planting a garden in the courtyard between the temple alcoves. Some laughed — nothing natural had grown in Drezen for seventy years. But Sister Elara supported the idea, and soon Asha was turning soil with a donated spade.

I helped her sometimes, during breaks from stonework. She taught me about soil preparation, about reading the health of earth by its color and texture. The ground here was gray and lifeless, but she mixed in blessed water and sang old farming songs while she worked.

"My grandmother always said that hope was the best fertilizer," she told me, carefully placing seeds in neat rows.

Three days later, the first green shoots appeared. Word spread through the camps like wildfire. People came just to stare at those tiny leaves — the first natural growth in Drezen since before their grandparents were born.

### A Brief Exchange

I had been working on installing windows in the Abadar alcove when I badly cut my hand on a shard of broken glass. Blood flowed freely, and the wound was deep enough to see bone. Brother Tomás was away, tending to sick refugees, so I wrapped it in cloth and continued working, not wanting to seem weak.

Varic appeared beside me so quietly I didn't notice until he spoke.

"That needs attention," he said simply.

"It's nothing. I can manage."

He gave me a look that suggested he'd heard such protestations many times before. "Pride in enduring unnecessary pain isn't holiness — it's stubbornness."

He took my injured hand in both of his. His touch was warm — warmer than normal healing magic. I felt the wound close, the pain fade, but more than that, I felt... understood. As if in that moment of contact, he saw exactly who I was — young, uncertain, trying too hard to prove myself worthy.

"You're doing good work here," he said quietly. "The windows you've installed catch the morning light perfectly. That matters more than you might think."

He started to move away, then paused. "Where are you from originally?"

"Egede. Just an acolyte. Nobody important."

"I was nobody important once too. The son of merchants who died on a trade route." He looked at the temple rising around us. "But unimportant people doing necessary work — that's how the world actually changes. Keep installing those windows. Let the light in."

And then he was gone, moving to help others, leaving me with a healed hand and the first real sense that I belonged here.

### The Night Market

By the end of the month, something extraordinary had evolved. Every evening, as construction wound down, an informal market would spring up around the temple. Refugees traded goods and services, soldiers spent their pay on small comforts, and the various faith communities mingled freely.

A priest of Desna taught children to juggle while their parents bartered for necessities. A dwarven smith devoted to Torag repaired tools in exchange for food. Sister Elara had convinced someone to string up lanterns between posts, creating pools of warm light that pushed back the oppressive darkness of the Worldwound.

I sold my services as a scribe, writing letters for those who couldn't write themselves. Through their words, I learned their stories — the carpenter who lost his wife at Kenabres, the mother searching for her scattered children, the soldier who just wanted his family to know he still lived.

This wasn't what any of us had expected when we came to help build a temple. We thought we were constructing a building. Instead, we were rebuilding civilization itself, one small transaction at a time.

### The Temple Rises

Now, as I write this one month after Drezen's liberation, the temple stands nearly complete. Eight alcoves for eight faiths, with Asha's garden growing impossibly green in the courtyard's center. The night market has become a permanent feature, with actual stalls being constructed.

I see Varic sometimes, moving through the crowds, healing the sick, blessing new ventures, mediating disputes. But mostly, he stays in the background, letting the community grow organically. He understood something we didn't at first — his role was to create the space and provide protection, but the actual rebuilding had to come from us.

Yesterday, a young tiefling woman set up a small school in one of the completed alcoves, teaching letters and numbers to anyone who wanted to learn. A former cultist, seeking redemption, helps Asha tend the garden with remarkable gentleness. The baker's daughter plays with the soldier's son while their parents work side by side.

This is what victory looks like — not grand gestures or divine fire, but ordinary people choosing to build something together despite their differences, despite their losses, despite the darkness pressing in from all sides.

### The Light We Kindle

I remain nameless in this account because I am everyone who came to Drezen seeking purpose and found community instead. We came to serve various gods and ended up serving each other. We came to build a temple and ended up building a home.

Tomorrow, the temple will be officially consecrated with representatives from Mendev in attendance. They'll see a magnificent structure honoring eight faiths. They'll see Sosiel Vaenic presiding over an unprecedented cooperation between traditionally separate churches.

But they won't see what really matters — the hundred small moments that brought us here. The refugee who shared her last bread with a stranger. The soldier who taught former enemies to read. The garden that shouldn't exist but does anyway, because someone decided to try.

This temple stands not because of any one person's power, but because ordinary people answered a call to build something in an impossible place. Every stone we laid, every window we installed, every meal we shared — these were acts of defiance against the corruption surrounding us.

I think about that brief moment when Varic healed my hand and told me that unimportant people doing necessary work is how the world changes. He was right. Not through great battles or divine intervention, but through choosing, day after day, to build rather than destroy, to trust rather than fear, to hope rather than despair.

The Worldwound still presses against our borders. Demons still howl in the night. But in this temple, in this growing community, light gathers — not the harsh light of divine fire, but the gentle, steady light of ordinary people refusing to let darkness win.

> "Grant us wisdom to know when to heal and when to cauterize, when to offer redemption and when to stand firm, when to be the gentle dawn and when to be the purifying flame. In balance, we find truth. In truth, we find service. In service, we find purpose." — Varic Sarian's daily prayer

*— Written by an unnamed acolyte of Sarenrae, Temple of United Faith, Drezen, one month after the Liberation —*
