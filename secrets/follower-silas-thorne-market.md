# Where Commerce Blooms

*A follower's account — **Silas Thorne**, cloth merchant of **Lupenor's Market** (the Celest House), written a month into the rebuilding of Drezen.*

### The Decision

I am Silas Thorne, a cloth merchant. Not a hero, not a crusader, just someone who knows fabric and thread. And numbers. Always numbers. Seven years in the trade. Four hundred and thirty-two different weave patterns memorized. Twelve suppliers lost to the war.

When news reached Nerosyan that Rabiah's Redeemers had taken Drezen, most sensible merchants shook their heads. Seventeen of them, actually — I counted as they gathered at the guildhall, dismissing the opportunity. A demon-haunted ruin was no place for trade. But then came different news — an elven slayer named Lupenor Celest was establishing a trading house. Three thousand gold pieces invested. Real money, real commitment.

The Celest name carried weight, even tainted as it was by rumors from Kenabres. But here was one of that family choosing to build something new in the most unlikely place. If someone with her connections believed Drezen could support commerce, perhaps there was opportunity there.

More importantly, my sister lived in a refugee camp near Egede with her three children. Ages seven, nine, and eleven — numbers that haunted my dreams. Her husband had died in the demon attacks — one of three hundred and sixteen casualties that day. If I could establish myself in Drezen, perhaps I could bring them somewhere they could start over. Somewhere defended by heroes who'd actually won.

So I loaded my cart with every bolt of cloth I owned — forty-three bolts, to be precise — hired two guards with my last silver, and joined a supply caravan heading north. Fifteen wagons, thirty-seven people, five-day journey. I counted every milestone.

### First Arrival

The Drezen I found was nothing like I'd imagined. Yes, it was ruined, but it was also alive with activity. Construction sounds echoed from every quarter. The Celest House at the southeastern foot of the walls was already taking shape — three stories of solid construction, forty-two windows, one main entrance, two side doors. I count when I'm nervous. It calms me.

I set up my cart in what had once been a market square, now mostly rubble with a few cleared spaces. Six other merchants were already there — a tinker, an herbalist, a woman selling salvaged tools, two provision sellers, and a cobbler. We eyed each other warily at first, competitors for limited coin.

Then an older merchant named Henrik, who'd been selling provisions, said something that changed everything: "Lady Celest says competition is healthy, but cooperation builds markets. We sink or swim together here."

Within an hour — sixty-three minutes, actually — we'd reorganized our carts to create a more inviting square. The herbalist's pleasant scents drew people to my colorful fabrics. My awning provided shade for the tool seller's wares. Small things, but it was a beginning.

### The Screening

After three days of modest sales — mostly mending work for soldiers — I was summoned to the Celest House. My stomach churned. Had I done something wrong? Was I being expelled?

A half-elven woman named Mira Thistledance interviewed me. Her questions seemed casual but I sensed deeper purpose. Where had I traded before? Who were my suppliers? Why come to such a dangerous place?

I answered honestly — my suppliers were small family operations in Nerosyan, I'd never traded anywhere significant, and I came because my sister needed somewhere safe to raise her children.

She made notes, then asked about several trading houses in Kenabres. I'd never heard of them, which seemed to please her.

"Lady Celest will review your application for a permanent stall," she said. "Continue your temporary setup for now."

I left confused but hopeful. There was more structure here than I'd expected, more planning. This wasn't just a military camp with some merchants. They were building something intentional.

### The Brief Moment

A week into my stay — seven days, one hundred and sixty-eight hours of growing hope — disaster struck. A drunk soldier accidentally knocked over a lantern near my cart. The fire spread quickly to my fabric inventory. Twenty-one bolts destroyed in less than three minutes. I fought it desperately with my cloak, but cloth burns so easily.

Other merchants rushed to help, forming a bucket brigade from the nearest well. Fourteen people, forty-seven buckets of water. The fire was controlled, but half my inventory was destroyed. I sat in the ashes of my hopes, calculating the losses. Four hundred and twenty silver pieces worth of cloth, gone. Without that inventory, I couldn't earn enough to establish myself, much less bring my sister here.

That's when she appeared — Lupenor Celest herself, moving through the market on some other business. She paused at the scene, taking in the damage with those sharp elven eyes. She counted too, I noticed — her fingers twitched slightly as she assessed. A fellow counter, perhaps, though hers was the calculation of a slayer measuring threats.

"Fire?" she asked simply.

"My own fault," I said quickly. "Poor placement of flammable goods."

She studied me for a moment. Two heartbeats. Three. "Merchant's guilt — blaming yourself for others' mistakes. Bad for negotiation, good for reputation." She turned to Mira. "Add them to the fire insurance pool we're establishing. Retroactive to yesterday."

Before I could respond, she was gone, continuing whatever business had brought her here. Seven seconds of conversation that changed everything. To her, it was probably a minor administrative decision, forgotten within the hour. To me, it was salvation. The insurance pool — funded by small contributions from all permanent merchants — would cover enough of my losses to continue.

### Building Trust

Word of the fire insurance spread quickly. Within days, more merchants arrived, seeing in this policy evidence that the Celest House was serious about protecting honest traders. We began organizing ourselves without being told to — establishing a night watch, creating a shared fund for wagon repairs, standardizing weights and measures.

A baker named Greta opened a stall selling bread that actually tasted like bread, not the hard rations we'd grown used to. A young couple started a laundry service. An old dwarf began repairing armor and weapons with remarkable skill.

We were becoming a real market, not just scattered traders. Soldiers got paid and spent their coin on small comforts. Refugees arrived with skills to trade if not money. Even the crusader commanders came down from the citadel to purchase supplies, lending legitimacy to our growing bazaar.

The Celest House provided structure — standard contracts, dispute mediation, quality verification — but never heavy-handed control. It felt like being part of something larger while still maintaining independence.

### The Letter

Three weeks after my arrival, I received approval for a permanent stall in the new covered market being built adjacent to the Celest House. The terms were fair — reasonable rent, protection from theft, access to the verified supplier network Lady Celest was establishing.

More importantly, permanent merchants could sponsor family members for residence permits.

I wrote to my sister that night:

> Dearest Sara, Drezen is not what you imagine. Yes, the walls are broken (fourteen major breaches, twenty-three minor) and demons howl at night (usually between the third and fifth hour). But children play in the market square during the day. I counted seventeen of them yesterday. There is fresh bread, clean water, and work for anyone willing. I have a permanent stall now — number thirty-seven in the covered market. Twelve feet by eight feet. Solid roof, stone walls, forty-two silver pieces monthly rent. The room above is small but dry — three hundred square feet, four windows. The school they're building will take all three children. It's not paradise. It's dangerous. But it's also alive in a way I haven't seen since before the war. Sixty-three permanent merchants now. Growing by the day. Come when you're ready. There's room for hope here. And I've measured it exactly. Your loving brother, Silas (who still counts everything twice).

### The Market at Month's End

Now, one month after Drezen's liberation, I stand in my permanent stall watching the evening market. Sixty-three registered merchants, hundreds of customers daily. The Celest House rises like a beacon at the southeastern foot of the walls, its windows glowing with lamplight as clerks work late recording transactions, verifying shipments, planning trade routes.

I see Lupenor Celest occasionally, usually in discussion with caravan masters or reviewing contracts. She probably doesn't remember the cloth merchant whose cart burned. But I remember her, and more importantly, I understand what she's building.

This isn't just about profit — though profit matters. It's about proving that civilization can take root anywhere, even in demon-haunted ruins. Every honest transaction, every fair deal, every merchant who chooses integrity over quick coin — we're all threads in a fabric she's weaving.

My sister arrives tomorrow with her children. I've hired a carpenter to partition the room above my stall, creating small but private spaces. The eldest boy is old enough to help with the shop. The younger ones will attend the new school. Sara's weaving will add another trade to our growing market.

Others are doing the same — bringing family, making plans beyond mere survival. The tinker is teaching apprentices. The baker is expanding, having sent for her cousin who knows pastry work. Henrik is organizing a merchants' guild with actual bylaws and elected positions.

### Where Commerce Blooms

We who trade in Drezen are not heroes. We didn't reclaim this citadel from demons or perform mighty deeds. We're simply people who saw opportunity where others saw only danger, who chose to build where others expected only war. One hundred and twelve of us now, permanent merchants and their families. I know because I count us every morning, a ritual that keeps the chaos at bay.

The Celest House gave us structure, protection, and most importantly, trust. In a land where corruption — literal and figurative — threatens everything, Lady Celest's obsessive verification and screening created something precious: a market where your word matters, where contracts are honored, where the coin isn't cursed.

I think about that moment when she authorized my inclusion in the fire insurance, seven seconds of her time that changed the trajectory of three lives — no, four, including myself. She probably makes a dozen such decisions daily, each one enabling someone else's fresh start. Twelve decisions, dozens of lives altered. The mathematics of hope.

This is how Drezen truly rises — not through grand gestures but through accumulated small acts of commerce and community. Every bolt of cloth sold, every loaf of bread baked, every fair trade completed adds another stone to the foundation of something permanent.

Tomorrow marks thirty-one days since I arrived. My sister's children will wake in their new home above a cloth merchant's stall for the first time. They'll hear the sounds of a living market — twenty-three cart wheels rolling by dawn, at least forty haggling voices by midday, five different hammers building something new. Normal sounds in an abnormal place. The sound of hope made manifest through commerce.

The demons still threaten. The Worldwound still corrupts. But here in this growing market, protected by heroes and enabled by a merchant's daughter who chose redemption over corruption, life continues. Trade flows. Families reunite. The future becomes possible, one transaction at a time.

And I count each one.

*— Account of Silas Thorne, cloth merchant, Stall Thirty-Seven, Southeastern Market, Celest House District, written one month after the Liberation of Drezen. May honest coin drive out corruption. —*
