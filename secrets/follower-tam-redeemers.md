# Following the Flame

*A follower's account — **Tam**, water-carrier and wall-builder of **Rabiah's Redeemers**, written a month into the rebuilding of Drezen.*

### How I Found Them

My name is Tam, and I came to Drezen chasing stories of the impossible. A sixteen-year-old sorceress who'd destroyed a corrupted wardstone. A ragtag group that had walked into a demon fortress and won through pure audacity. They called them Rabiah's Redeemers, though from what I heard, organization wasn't exactly their strength.

I was conscripted into a supply wagon in Nerosyan — caught stealing bread, if you must know — but when I heard we were delivering to Drezen, my punishment became opportunity. The moment we passed through the gates, I slipped away.

Finding their camp was easy. While the crusaders had neat rows of identical tents and the merchants had orderly stalls, Rabiah's followers had... well, it looked like a festival had collided with a refugee camp and decided to stay. Bright fabrics stretched between posts at odd angles. A half-built structure that might have been a kitchen leaned against another half-built structure that could have been storage. Children chased chickens between tent ropes while someone practiced juggling badly.

An older woman with herb-stained fingers spotted me hovering at the edge. "Lost or looking?" she asked.

"Looking. Is this really where the Redeemers camp?"

She laughed. "Camp, live, argue about whose turn it is to cook. I'm Tilda Merrow. I keep these fools from dying of their own enthusiasm."

### The Old Guard

The first thing I learned was that there were two types of followers: those who'd been there since Kenabres and those who'd arrived after. The old guard moved through the chaos with practiced ease, like they'd learned to navigate by different stars than the rest of us.

Sir Brannick — who wasn't really a knight but had beautiful handwriting — tried to explain the camp's structure my first evening. We sat around a cooking fire built from three different attempts at a proper hearth.

"You see," he said, gesturing at the architectural disaster surrounding us, "Marcus started building a communal kitchen over there. Then Elena decided we needed a covered eating area, so she started building onto it. But she built north while Marcus was building east, so now we have an L-shaped thing that's neither kitchen nor dining hall."

"What is it then?" I asked.

"Tuesday's mistake. Wednesday we started over there." He pointed to another half-structure. "That was going to be a bathhouse until someone remembered we need to eat more than we need to bathe."

A halfling woman nearby chuckled. Cornelia Dewfoot, recently arrived but somehow already indispensable. "Oh, but isn't it marvelous how organic it all is?" she gushed. "Such creative energy! Our dear Rabiah must be so proud of how we're growing!"

Tilda snorted. "Rabiah barely notices unless something's on fire. And even then, only if it's not supposed to be."

### Building the Impossible

Within three days, I was hauling salvaged timber for what someone optimistically called "the common house." The plan — if you could call it that — was to create one large building with communal spaces. Kitchen, eating area, gathering space, some basic sleeping quarters for those without tents.

The problem was that everyone had ideas and no one had authority.

"The cooking fires should face east," insisted a former innkeeper.

"West!" argued a camp cook. "Morning sun in your eyes while cooking?"

"We need more storage," someone else added, already nailing boards where the doorway was supposed to go.

What emerged was... unique. The kitchen had three different ceiling heights because three different people had started building simultaneously. The common room was actually two rooms that someone had knocked a hole between. The sleeping quarters were above the kitchen, below the storage, and somehow also beside the washroom.

A visiting merchant looked at our construction and asked, "What style of architecture is this?"

"Enthusiasm," Brannick replied dryly.

### Glimpses of Her

I'd been there a week before I saw Rabiah properly. She emerged from her tent — a perfectly normal tent surrounded by the chaos we'd created — reading a book while walking, sparks of wild magic trailing from her fingers.

She was so young. Younger than me, younger than half her followers. Sixteen years old with dark hair in a messy braid and robes that had seen better days. But power rolled off her in waves, casual and unconscious, like heat from a forge.

She looked up at our architectural monstrosity, tilted her head, and said, "Huh. It's taller."

Then she went back to reading and wandered toward the market district, leaving us all staring after her like we'd been blessed.

"She noticed!" someone whispered excitedly.

"She said it was taller!"

"That's practically approval!"

Cornelia watched with those too-innocent eyes of hers. "Such wisdom in one so young," she murmured. "To see the growth where others see only chaos."

Tilda gave her a look I couldn't interpret, but said nothing.

### The Kitchen Incident

The day we finally got the kitchen functional was a celebration. We'd cobbled together three fire pits, two prep areas, and something that might charitably be called a pantry. It could feed sixty people, assuming those people weren't too picky about eating in shifts and didn't mind that the smoke went sideways instead of up.

I was carrying water buckets when Rabiah appeared in the doorway — or what we'd decided was the main doorway that week. She looked around at our chaos, then at the pot of stew I'd just knocked over in surprise at seeing her.

"Sorry!" I squeaked.

She blinked at me, then at the spreading puddle of stew. "Is it supposed to be on the floor?"

"No! I just — you startled — I'm sorry, Lady Rabiah!"

"Lady?" She looked genuinely confused. "I'm sixteen. Ladies are people who know which fork to use." She paused. "Actually, Brannick knows which fork to use. Maybe he's a lady."

She wandered to the cooking pot, peered inside, and said, "Needs salt." Then she left, having turned my disaster into the most important moment of my life.

The stew did need salt.

### The Community

What we lacked in organization, we made up for in enthusiasm. The compound grew daily — not according to any plan, but like a living thing. Someone would start adding a wall, someone else would add a roof, a third person would decide that spot needed a window.

We helped everywhere in Drezen. When the temple builders needed extra hands, twenty of us showed up. When the merchants needed guards, we volunteered. When refugee children needed distraction, we became entertainment. We were everywhere and nowhere, filling gaps nobody else even saw.

The paladins next door hated us. Our singing at midnight, our construction at dawn, our general existence at all hours. But even they couldn't deny we got things done. Messily, chaotically, but done.

One evening, Sir Brannick gathered the newer arrivals. "You want to know what she's really like?" he asked. "Rabiah?"

We nodded eagerly.

"She's sixteen years old and she argued a mythic demon into doubting its own plan. She channels more magic accidentally than most mages do on purpose. She forgets to eat unless reminded, stays up three nights straight reading, and once set her own tent on fire because she was thinking about theoretical resonances."

He smiled at our awestruck faces. "She's not a general or a prophet or a leader in any traditional sense. She's a brilliant, chaotic, impossibly powerful teenager who looked at the world and decided the rules were more like suggestions. And somehow, we all decided to follow."

### The Speech from Afar

Last night, something magical happened. Rabiah climbed onto our ramshackle building's somehow-stable roof. Word spread like wildfire — she was going to speak! We gathered in our dozens, cramming into the courtyard we'd accidentally created by building in a circle.

She stood above us, silhouetted against the evening sky, sparks dancing around her fingers. Sixteen years old, younger than half of us, more powerful than all of us combined.

"So," she began, and even her casual words carried weight, "apparently we're building a thing."

Nervous laughter rippled through the crowd.

"It doesn't match. The walls are crooked. I'm pretty sure that corner defies several laws of physics." She paused. "It's perfect."

The cheer that went up probably annoyed every paladin in Drezen.

"This is what we are," she continued. "We're not the ordered ranks or the merchant guilds or the holy builders. We're the chaos that fills the cracks. We're the ones who look at impossible and laugh. We're the ones too stubborn or too stupid to quit."

Magic flared around her — not controlled, just pure expression.

"They say we're disorganized. They're right. They say we're chaos. Absolutely. They say we can't possibly succeed like this." She grinned, and for a moment she looked exactly what she was — a teenager who'd discovered the universe's cheat codes. "Bet you we can."

As she climbed down, I caught Cornelia watching with an expression I couldn't read. For just a second, the halfling's mask of simple devotion slipped, revealing something sharper, more calculating. Then she was cheering with the rest of us, eyes wide with manufactured wonder.

### Following the Flame

A month in, and I finally understand. We don't follow Rabiah because she leads us. We follow because she gives us permission to be ourselves. Chaotic, messy, imperfect selves building something impossible together.

Our compound looks like it was designed by committee — because it was. By sixty different committees that never met. It has no coherent style, no unified plan, and definitely no structural integrity. But it's ours. Every wonky wall, every impossible angle, every room that shouldn't exist but does.

Tomorrow, more will arrive. They'll come seeking the hero who destroyed the wardstone, and they'll find a sixteen-year-old girl reading while walking into walls. They'll find Tilda keeping us fed, Brannick pretending we're organized, and Cornelia being suspiciously helpful while maintaining her facade of simple devotion.

They'll find a building that shouldn't stand but does, a community that shouldn't work but does, and a way of life that makes no sense except to those living it.

And if they're lucky — if they're crazy enough — they'll pick up a hammer and add their own impossible angle to our beautiful disaster.

Because that's what we are: Rabiah's Redeemers. Not because she redeemed us, but because she let us know we didn't need redemption. We just needed permission to be chaotic together.

The compound grows daily. Drezen rises around us. And somewhere in her tent, a sixteen-year-old sorceress reads magical theory, occasionally remembering we exist, blessing us with benign neglect while we build something gloriously, perfectly wrong.

*— Scrawled by Tam, water-carrier, wall-builder, and proud contributor to the architectural disaster; the Compound of Chaos, one month after Liberation. P.S. — She was right about the salt. —*
